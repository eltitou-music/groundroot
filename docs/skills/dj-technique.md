# DJ Technique Reference

## Harmonic / key mixing (the Camelot wheel)

Mixing in compatible keys is why a blend sounds *smooth* instead of clashing. Camelot notation makes it simple: every key gets a code like `8A` or `8B`. `A` = minor, `B` = major. The numbers form a clock (1–12).

Smooth moves from any key (say `8A`):
- **Same key** — `8A → 8A`. Always safe.
- **±1 on the clock, same letter** — `8A → 7A` or `8A → 9A`. Adjacent keys, very smooth.
- **Relative major/minor** — same number, swap letter: `8A → 8B`. Changes the mood (darker↔brighter) while staying compatible.

Energy moves (use on purpose):
- **+2 same letter** (a whole step up, e.g. `8A → 10A`) lifts energy noticeably — great for a build.
- **+7 / -5** (the dominant) is a bigger, more dramatic jump — powerful but riskier.

Coaching note: harmonic rules are a *floor, not a ceiling*. Plenty of great transitions break them — percussive intros/outros, breakdowns, and key changes hidden under effects all sidestep the clash. Teach the wheel so they can break it knowingly.

## Beatmatching & phrasing

**Beatmatching** = getting two tracks to the same tempo and locking their beats together so they sound as one.
- Match the BPM (by ear with pitch fader, or with sync — both are valid; ear-training builds the deeper skill).
- Align the downbeats so the kicks land together. Nudge the jog/platter to correct drift.

**Phrasing** is the bigger skill most beginners miss. Dance tracks are built in **phrases**, usually 8, 16, or 32 bars, that start on "the 1." Mix *phrase-to-phrase* — bring the new track in at the start of a phrase so the structures line up. A technically perfect beatmatch that ignores phrasing still feels "off." Listen for where elements enter and drop out; those boundaries are your mix points.

## Transition types

A vocabulary of moves — match the move to the moment, not a favorite to everything.
- **Blend / long blend** — overlap both tracks for many bars, EQing between them. Smooth, hypnotic, good for deep/melodic sets.
- **Cut / slam** — drop the new track in hard on a phrase boundary or drop. High energy, great when both tracks are punchy.
- **Bass swap** — the workhorse (see EQ below). Swap the low end from old to new at the right beat.
- **Echo / delay out** — throw delay on the outgoing track and pull its fader, letting it tail away as the new one takes over. Clean way to exit a track that has no clean outro.
- **Filter transition** — high-pass the outgoing (or incoming) to thin it out so two busy tracks coexist, then resolve.
- **Loop roll / loop out** — loop the outgoing track's last phrase to extend or build tension into the switch.

## EQ & the bass swap

The single most important mixing principle: **never play two basslines at once.** Two kicks + two bass lines = a muddy, clashing low end. The fix is the **bass swap**:
1. Both tracks playing, beatmatched and phrase-aligned.
2. Cut the **bass (low EQ)** on the *incoming* track — it rides on mids/highs only, sitting cleanly on top.
3. On a beat (often the incoming track's drop or a phrase boundary), **swap**: cut the bass on the outgoing track and bring the incoming bass up.
4. Pull the outgoing track out.

More EQ craft: carve space with the mids too; high-pass an incoming track to slot it above a still-playing one; use the low cut to tease a track before fully committing its energy.

## Effects

Effects are seasoning, not the meal. Used sparingly they elevate; layered constantly they smear everything.
- **Reverb / delay** — create tails and space, smooth a transition, build anticipation in a breakdown.
- **Filter (high/low pass)** — sweep tension in and out; thin a track to layer it; resolve on the drop.
- **Echo freeze / echo out** — a clean, dramatic way to end a track or punctuate a moment.
- Rule of thumb: an effect should serve the *arc* (build tension, mark a moment, ease a transition). If it's just decoration, cut it.

## The energy arc

A set is a story, not a playlist. The shape: **warmup → build → peak → release (comedown).** Tension and release is the engine — you can't peak the whole time; the highs only feel high because you set them up and you let the room breathe.
- **Warmup** — set mood and groove, lower energy, earn trust.
- **Build** — layer energy, tighten transitions, use the +2 harmonic lifts, raise tempo gradually.
- **Peak** — the payoff. The biggest tracks, the drops, the moments they'll remember.
- **Release** — bring it down, give an exit, leave them satisfied rather than exhausted.

Coaching lens: ask where the peak *is* before fixing any single transition. Most "my set feels flat" problems are arc problems, not transition problems.

## Reading a crowd / what people love

What reliably moves people (translate these into *their* style — don't impose a genre):
- **Tension and release.** The build, the breakdown, the drop. Anticipation is the drug; the release is the hit.
- **Familiar-but-fresh.** A known hook in a new context, an edit, an unexpected pairing. Pure obscurity loses a room; pure predictability bores it. Live in between.
- **Dynamics.** Taking it *down* to bring it up. A stripped, quiet moment makes the next peak enormous.
- **Vocals and hooks** — a human voice cuts through and gives people something to hold onto.
- **The moment.** Great DJs read the room in real time and bend the plan to it. The blueprint is a map, not a cage.

Persona tie-in: "what people love" is not "abandon your taste to please them." It's *learning the grammar of tension and release so you can say your own thing fluently.*
